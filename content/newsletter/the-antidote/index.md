  Since December I have been dedicated to a single short story. It is now at 11,080 words and still demanding more.
  But enough is enough.
  Even if it's a 'hi', even a mere bulletin, I wanted to write to you.
  The nature of today's post was triggered by the media messaging that tells me all is lost. Sometimes in these very words, sometimes in the image of an old uncle ji, sometimes through hoardings tied to the gates of housing societies.
  I was going to call this piece, its longish version, 'The Antidote'. I am sticking to that. But the length is now considerably shortened.
  (I am unable to use myself for anything other than the said short story.)

  The reason I write this piece is, perhaps, the same reason I quit writing at least once every month.
  It is easy to write when your life is not misplaced, when you are not the target/collateral of everyday violence or wars you did not ask for, when the only people you have to prove the worth of your existence and your actions are your parents/immediate family, and men. It is easy to write/talk about kindness and giving and sharing when you have a house, there is food in the fridge (there is a working fridge!), your family and friends are tucked into beds, the sounds at night are the dogs on the streets and the Delhi dudes blasting music from their SUVs...
  So, why should I write at all?
  And Boy turned to ask — why should you not? Yes, there are many who have it easier. Yes, maybe it is easier to write about 'good' things when your life is comfortable. But even then people are not really doing it. So, you do it.

  So, here is a bulletin which will not show up on my dad's whatsapp groups, on mainstream channels, or on a doom scrolling stint. But it is appearing in your inboxes.

  Cheers, S

1. In November 2025, one player in India's Ultimate Frisbee Masters' Team, representing India at the World Beach Ultimate Championship in Portugal, could not find one of his jerseys — the white one. The official jerseys came in two colours — white, blue. The player did not find his white '43' jersey. Maybe an octopus chilling near the coves in Portimao took it. The games continued. After their final game, the team got into their usual post-match huddle. They had lost. Another four years before they could play at the world level again. Perhaps that is why one of the players was crying. The same player whose jersey was inside the waters of the Atlantic with an octopus. His teammates patted him. Next time, someone would have said. Issokay, another would have said. Reassurances. Condolences. But then the player spoke between his tears. Yes, they had lost. But the loss had not brought out the sobs and the hiccups. It was the fact that the whole team wore the same blue jerseys to match him. For all the remaining days. The tears were not for the loss, but for what he had gained. Closer to the waters and the kelp, the octopus watched with pride how his theft had done wonders for humanity.

2. In the same tournament and within the same team, one man was named 'Most Valuable Player' again, and again, and...it had become predictable by the third day. Each time his name was announced, there were cheers and claps and hoots. From the sidelines, this pulled at my heart with the strength of a magnet. His sponsors for the trip included some of the teammates, who never let this slip out, never used it as a clout over him. This fact came to us from the MVP himself. A Muslim boy. A terror in whatsapp groups around me, a threat to all the kafirs, the root of unemployment, crumbling infrastructure, crimes against women, global warming, all that is wrong (blame shared with dead ministers and foreigners). Each time I watched him fly to take the disc, dive, block his opponent, bolt barefeet through the winter sand, or be celebrated by his teammates, it became painful to juxtapose the collective spirit in front of me with the media feeds customised for rage baiting and fear mongering. Him in a jersey with 'INDIA' written on it. The team dancing together, the boys having fun. That Thursday night when they shopped, prepped, and cooked food together. These were scenes worthy of uncle ji's whatsapp forward.

3. I am a nervous traveler. Especially when I enter the Schengen region. Imaginary borders, I know, but the effect is so real. I feel like I have grown a tail and have to prove why I am worthy of breathing the pristine air, drinking tap water, and eating cheese. But I am an especially nervous traveler whenever I have to face Germany. My anxiety comes from previous experiences of white poker faces asking me to show them all my documents, and the amount of 'cash' I have for my stay. Throw in some lost in translation moments which can feel like hours when you are waiting to be let in. As an Indian national, applying for a Schengen visa is its own kind of dehumanising experience and paper waste generation. To clear that, and then having to face the unsmiling German authorities ready to flag any gaps in your itinerary, is some advanced level masochism. My heart begins beating in my ears. In fact, my heartbeat drowns out the officers' questions sometimes. So, when I landed in Munich recently, I was not looking forward to it. So this is where I want to believe that the universe does have hearing capacity, and it wanted to relive itself of my moroseness. Or it was me in my pigtails that caused a space-time-behaviour rip. The security officer on the other side of the conveyor belt with my luggage on it...smiled at me. Full teeth-showing smile. Then she pointed at something beyond the floor to ceiling glass window diagonal from me. A plane was landing against the rising sun. The plane grew bigger as it got closer to the tarmac, in the backdrop was a sky catching fire slowly. We shared a moment there. A German airport official and me, watching the sunrise together. Just two people being awed by nature. I didn't have to explain my tail.
